## Source Position

**What kind of source is this?**
A practical Jungian clinical handbook written by two analysts (Whitmont, Perera) for clinicians. The framing is explicitly clinical throughout: the reader is a therapist; the dreamer is a patient/analysand. Block 1 establishes the epistemological and practical frame for all subsequent chapters: what dreams are, how interpretation works, and what the "situation as it is" principle means.

**How the authors frame the book:**
Not as a theory manual but as a "circle, each chapter providing a way station from which to focus on the dream, which lies at its center." The approach is multi-perspectival: symbolic/allegoric, personal associative, collective/mythological, dramatic structure, body, and transferential. All must "hover" in the practitioner's consciousness. The tone is humble: "no method leads to infallible result" (citing Jung CW); the first rule is "paradox."

**How it relates to Jung:**
Directly Jungian — uses CW extensively, capitalizes Self, employs Jung's vocabulary (compensation, amplification, archetype, individuation). But more procedural: takes Jung's scattered methodological statements and organizes them into a step-by-step workflow. Acknowledges that psychoanalytic community is "rediscovering" Jung without crediting him.

**How it differs from Jung Dreams (SRC-SIMB-011):**
Jung Dreams was theoretical essays + extensive case material organized by era. SRC-SIMB-012 is organized by METHOD, not theory. It is more operational. It foregrounds the dreamer's participation and assent more explicitly. It introduces the "Guiding Self" as a specific technical term to distinguish from psychoanalytic "self." The "situation as it is" principle and the drama structure analysis are more developed here than in Jung's CW.

**What makes it practical:**
- Clear step-by-step orientation (Ch. 5-9)
- Case examples illustrating each failure mode
- Explicit rules ("In no case is pushing warranted"; "Nothing in dream outcome is to be regarded as fixed")
- Named procedures for each phase of work

**What makes it clinically risky:**
- Assumes a therapeutic container (dyadic analytic relationship)
- Describes clinical interventions, transference handling, case management decisions
- Prognosis/diagnosis language pervasive from Ch. 2
- Active imagination, Gestalt, psychodrama described as legitimate methods
- Fragile/fragmented ego handling requires clinical authority Iris does not have
